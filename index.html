How living systems make minerals without a furnace.

Biomineralization is the process by which organisms form mineral-containing structures. The core idea is simple: life does not break the rules of chemistry; it controls the local rules of the game.

Cells and tissues tune ion supply, pH, water, organic matrices, confinement, enzymes, and time so that minerals appear in the right place, with the right shape, at the right scale.

A biomineral can form through several linked stages: dissolved ions, ion-association complexes, amorphous or liquid-like precursors, particle attachment, phase transformation, and crystal growth.

Start here

What is biomineralization?

Biomineralization is mineral formation under biological influence. In the simplest case, biological activity changes the local environment until a mineral precipitates. In the more controlled case, cells actively regulate when, where, and how a mineral forms [1–4].

Beginner

Living organisms can make hard parts such as shells, bones, teeth, and coral skeletons by controlling where mineral crystals form.

Intermediate / advanced

Biomineralization includes biologically induced and biologically controlled mineral formation. It is governed by thermodynamics, kinetics, ion transport, organic-inorganic interfaces, solution chemistry, and biological regulation [1–4,18].

Most biominerals are not pure crystals. They are hierarchical organic-inorganic composites: an inorganic mineral phase reinforced, shaped, or regulated by biological molecules such as collagen, chitin, acidic proteins, glycoproteins, polysaccharides, lipids, or extracellular polymeric substances [1–4,18].

The important beginner point is this: a shell, bone, or tooth is not just “rock made by an organism.” It is a structured material that uses mineral chemistry plus biological architecture [1–4].

Biomineral

A mineral-containing structure made by, or strongly influenced by, a living system.

Organic matrix

The biological scaffold and additive package that can bind ions, template surfaces, block crystal faces, or confine reaction spaces.

Biologically induced

Mineral formation caused indirectly by metabolism, for example when microbes change pH or carbonate chemistry.

Biologically controlled

Mineral formation regulated by cells, genes, tissues, transport proteins, vesicles, and extracellular matrices.

Examples include calcium carbonate in mollusc shells and corals, calcium phosphate in vertebrate bones and teeth, silica in diatoms and sponges, magnetite in magnetotactic bacteria, and iron minerals in several microbial and animal systems [1–4].

The cast of characters

What ingredients does a biomineralizing system need?

Biomineralization usually needs four things: ions, a fluid environment, a place where nucleation can occur, and a way to control growth. The fluid is often water-rich, but its chemistry can be highly regulated in tiny spaces such as vesicles, extracellular compartments, collagen gaps, shell-forming spaces, or microbial biofilms [1–4,18].

Beginner

A biomineral needs dissolved building blocks, such as calcium and carbonate or phosphate, plus a local environment that lets those building blocks assemble.

Intermediate / advanced

The relevant variables include ion activities, pH, carbonate or phosphate speciation, saturation state, interfacial free energy, macromolecular binding, transport, confinement, and transformation pathways [5–8,18].

1

Ions

Ca²⁺, CO₃²⁻, HCO₃⁻, PO₄³⁻, HPO₄²⁻, Mg²⁺, Sr²⁺, F⁻, Na⁺ and others can affect phase, shape, solubility, and defect chemistry.

2

Water + pH

Water hydrates ions and surfaces. pH controls carbonate and phosphate speciation, which changes supersaturation and mineral identity.

3

Organic matrices

Collagen, chitin, proteins, polysaccharides, and EPS can concentrate ions, stabilize precursors, or inhibit unwanted growth.

Carbonate and phosphate chemistry are especially pH-sensitive. In carbonate systems, dissolved carbon dioxide, bicarbonate, and carbonate interconvert. In phosphate systems, phosphoric acid species interconvert. Biology can therefore steer mineralization by controlling pH and ion transport rather than by directly “placing” every atom [1–4,7,18].

⚗️

Thermodynamics without fear

Why do minerals form at all?

A mineral forms when moving from dissolved ions to a solid lowers the relevant free energy enough to make the process favorable. The most useful bookkeeping term is Gibbs free energy, G. A process is thermodynamically favorable when the change is negative: ΔG < 0 [5,6].

Beginner

Gibbs free energy is a way of asking whether a process is energetically favorable overall. If ΔG is negative, the final state is favored, but that does not mean the process happens instantly.

Intermediate / advanced

For nucleation, the total free-energy change is often described as the sum of a favorable volume or bulk term and an unfavorable interfacial term. The maximum in this free-energy curve corresponds to the critical nucleus [5,6].

Enthalpy, H, is the heat-content part of the story. Making strong interactions in a crystal can lower enthalpy. But crystallization also affects entropy, S: ions become more ordered, hydration shells change, and water molecules may be released. Gibbs free energy combines these contributions: ΔG = ΔH − TΔS [5,6].

Why there is a barrier even when crystallization is favorable

Tiny clusters have a high surface-area-to-volume ratio. The positive interfacial free-energy term competes with the negative bulk free-energy term. A stable nucleus appears only after the cluster passes a critical size.

Supersaturation

The solution contains more mineral-forming ions than would be stable at equilibrium. This is the driving force for precipitation.

Ion activity product

A concentration-like measure of the reacting ions, corrected for non-ideal solution behavior.

Solubility product, Ksp

The equilibrium value that separates undersaturation, saturation, and supersaturation for a given phase.

Interfacial energy

The energetic cost of making a boundary between a new mineral and surrounding solution, matrix, or tissue.

Organisms often control mineralization by shifting the balance: increasing local supersaturation, lowering interfacial free energy with a template, stabilizing a metastable precursor, or slowing growth until the desired shape is achieved [3–8,18].

⚛️

Crystallisation theories

How does a crystal start?

Classical nucleation theory treats crystal formation as a competition between a favorable bulk free-energy term and an unfavorable interfacial free-energy term. Ions add to a cluster. If the cluster is too small, it dissolves. If it reaches a critical size, growth becomes more likely [5,6].

Beginner

A crystal does not usually appear fully formed. It starts as a tiny cluster. Very small clusters are unstable; larger clusters can become stable nuclei.

Intermediate / advanced

Classical nucleation theory expresses this as a free-energy barrier controlled by supersaturation, interfacial free energy, and nucleus size. Biomineralizing systems can alter this barrier through surfaces, additives, confinement, and precursor stabilization [5–8,18].

That picture is useful but incomplete. Many biomineral and biomimetic systems also use non-classical pathways: ion-association complexes, prenucleation clusters, dense liquid-like intermediates, amorphous nanoparticles, oriented attachment, mesocrystals, and transformation from a hydrated precursor into a more ordered crystal [5–8].

A

Classical route

Individual ions or molecular units add to a nucleus and then to a growing crystal face.

B

Amorphous precursor route

A disordered, often hydrated phase forms first, then dehydrates and crystallizes later.

C

Particle attachment route

Nanoparticles attach, align, fuse, and reorganize into a larger crystal or mesocrystal.

No single crystallization model explains all biomineralization systems. Different organisms, tissues, solution chemistries, matrices, and degrees of confinement can favor different mineralization pathways [5–8,18].

Biology as a materials engineer

How does biology steer mineral formation?

How an organic matrix can create a preferred nucleation site

This simplified three-step diagram shows the main idea: ions are first dispersed in solution, then Ca²⁺ binds to negatively charged sites on an organic matrix. Phosphate ions can associate with the locally concentrated Ca²⁺ ions, increasing local supersaturation near the surface. When supersaturation is high enough, a mineral nucleus can form preferentially at the matrix.

Living systems control mineralization with a toolkit rather than a single trick. Important tools include ion transport, pH regulation, macromolecular binding, confinement, enzyme activity, and inhibition of unwanted growth [1–4,18].

Beginner

Biology can decide where minerals form by changing the local chemistry around a matrix or tissue surface.

Intermediate / advanced

Organic matrices and interfaces can change ion activities, hydration, interfacial free energy, precursor stability, crystallographic orientation, and growth rates. Their effect depends on concentration, conformation, pH, competing ions, and surface chemistry [3,4,18].

Important controls include ion transport, pH regulation, macromolecular binding, confinement, enzymes, and inhibition. Preventing unwanted phases or uncontrolled precipitation is as important as promoting the desired mineral.

CaCO₃ systems

What is special about calcium carbonate biominerals?

Calcium carbonate biominerals occur in mollusc shells, coral skeletons, sea urchin spines, coccoliths, eggshells, crustacean cuticles, otoliths, and otoconia. Their formula is simple, CaCO₃, but their biological forms are highly diverse [1–4,9–12].

Beginner

Calcium carbonate is the main mineral in many shells and skeletons. The same chemical formula can appear in different crystal forms.

Intermediate / advanced

Calcium carbonate biomineralization involves polymorph selection among calcite, aragonite, vaterite, and amorphous calcium carbonate. Selection depends on solution chemistry, Mg/Ca ratio, pH, organic macromolecules, confinement, and precursor stabilization [9–12,18].

Calcite is often the most stable calcium carbonate polymorph under ambient conditions, but organisms commonly produce aragonite or vaterite-like structures when those are functionally useful. Magnesium, organic molecules, confinement, pH, and precursor stabilization can all influence which form appears [3,4,9–12,18].

Amorphous calcium carbonate, or ACC, is a disordered precursor found or proposed in many carbonate biomineralization pathways. It can be moldable, hydrated, and easier to transport or shape before it transforms into a crystalline phase. Organisms and microbes may stabilize ACC using magnesium, phosphate, organic molecules, or extracellular polymeric substances [9–12].

CaP systems

What is special about calcium phosphate biominerals?

Calcium phosphate is central to vertebrate hard tissues. Bone, dentin, cementum, and enamel are mineralized mainly with apatite-like calcium phosphate, but biological apatite is usually not perfect stoichiometric hydroxyapatite. It is commonly carbonate-substituted, ion-substituted, calcium-deficient, nanoscale, and defect-rich [13–17,26–29].

Beginner

Bones and teeth are not made of pure mineral. They are biological composites in which calcium phosphate mineral is organized with collagen, water, cells, or enamel proteins.

Intermediate / advanced

Biological apatite is typically carbonated, non-stoichiometric, nanoscale, and structurally integrated with organic matrices. Its composition and morphology influence solubility, mechanics, remodeling, and spectroscopic signatures [13–17,26–29].

Calcium phosphate mineralization can involve amorphous calcium phosphate (ACP), octacalcium phosphate-like environments, tricalcium phosphate phases in some contexts, and apatite-like final products. In biological tissues, the route may include ion-association complexes and precursor phases before mature apatite appears [7,13–17,39].

Bone and dentin are collagen-rich composites with nanoscale apatite and significant water and organic content. Enamel is much more highly mineralized and contains long, organized apatite crystals, giving it high hardness but less toughness and less capacity for repair [13–17,26–29].

Carbonate, magnesium, sodium, fluoride, hydrogen phosphate, and vacancies can change crystal size, solubility, lattice parameters, mechanical behavior, and biological remodeling. That is why “hydroxyapatite” is often a convenient shorthand rather than a complete description of biological mineral [13–17,26,39].

Structure across scales

Why are biominerals often stronger or tougher than simple minerals?

Biominerals are organized across length scales. Atomic substitutions influence lattice behavior. Nanocrystal size affects solubility and strength. Organic-mineral interfaces dissipate energy. Larger architectures such as nacre tablets, collagen fibrils, enamel rods, or coral skeletons guide cracks and distribute stress [1–4,14–17,26–29].

Beginner

Biominerals are strong because structure matters. They are built like organized composites, not like random piles of crystals.

Intermediate / advanced

Mechanical function arises from hierarchical organization, mineral-organic interfaces, anisotropy, defect distribution, hydration, crystal size, and tissue architecture. These features affect stiffness, toughness, damage tolerance, and remodeling [1–4,14–17,26–29].

This hierarchy helps explain why nacre can be tougher than geological aragonite, why bone can be stiff yet remodelable, and why enamel can resist wear. The mineral phase supplies stiffness and hardness; the organic phase and architecture help control defects, cracks, hydration, and repair [1–4,14–17,26–29,39–40].

Medicine, archaeology, and heritage

How does biomineralization connect medicine, archaeology, and cultural heritage?

Bone and teeth are living biominerals during life, but they can also become archives after death. Their mineral and organic components record growth, remodeling, chemistry, damage, disease, diet, and environmental exposure. For this reason, the same materials-science tools used to study biomineralization can also answer questions in medicine, archaeology, and cultural heritage.

In medicine and dentistry, biomineralization helps explain normal tissue formation, pathological mineralization, bone fragility, enamel defects, caries, dental calculus, tissue repair, and implant integration. Bone mineral density alone is not enough: mineral crystal size, carbonate substitution, collagen quality, water, porosity, and hierarchical organization all influence mechanical performance and disease state.

Forensic applications often use human skeletal and dental tissues to answer medico-legal questions. Teeth and bones can help estimate age, identify remains, reconstruct geographic origin, evaluate trauma, and preserve chemical or molecular traces.

Archaeological bone and teeth can preserve information about diet, mobility, disease, growth, weaning, craft practices, animal management, and burial environment. Dental calculus, which is mineralized plaque, can trap microremains and biomolecules, making it useful for studying oral microbiomes, diet, environmental exposure, and sometimes individual life histories.

Cultural heritage collections include bone, ivory, antler, horn, shell, coral, pearls, eggshell, parchment, and mineralized archaeological remains. Understanding biomineral structure helps conservators identify materials, assess deterioration, distinguish original tissue from diagenetic alteration, choose appropriate storage conditions, and avoid destructive sampling when non-invasive methods can answer the question.

Diverse research contexts

How is biomineralization studied in diverse contexts?

Biomineralization is relevant across fields because mineralized tissues are both functional biological materials and archives of chemical, structural, and environmental information. Bone, teeth, shells, and other mineralized tissues can therefore be studied to understand tissue formation, disease, repair, preservation, identity, mobility, diet, and past environments [22–44].

Bone as a hierarchical material

Mineral organization, tissue quality, and interfaces

Bone is a calcium phosphate-based composite in which carbonated apatite, collagen, water, porosity, and cellular remodeling are organized across length scales. Research in this area examines bone mineral morphology, Raman-derived compositional metrics, collagen-mineral organization, osteocyte lacunae, pathological mineral phases, scanning electron microscopy of bone, and bone-implant interfaces [26–29,39–44].

Bone cannot be understood as a simple hydroxyapatite block. Its function depends on how apatite-like mineral forms, transforms, ages, mineralizes collagen, and integrates with biological or implant interfaces [14–17,26–29,39–44].

Medical and forensic imaging

Reading internal structure without destroying the sample

Radiography, micro-CT, and related imaging methods can reveal skeletal structure, fractures, developmental features, dental tissues, pathological changes, and preservation state. These approaches are important when remains are rare, sensitive, clinically relevant, or legally significant [30–32].

The contrast seen in imaging comes from mineral density, tissue architecture, porosity, and the organization of biomineralized structures. Imaging therefore connects medical diagnosis, forensic interpretation, and archaeological conservation [19–21,30–32].

Teeth as preserved archives

Development, DNA preservation, and life-history signals

Teeth contain enamel, dentine, cementum, and pulp spaces, each with different mineral content, formation timing, remodeling behavior, and preservation potential. Research on permanent and deciduous teeth can help evaluate which tissues preserve DNA, chemical signatures, or developmental information [23–25,33–34].

Tooth tissues form in stages and remodel differently. Their mineralization history affects what biological, forensic, and archaeological information can be recovered later [22–25,33–34].

Burnt and altered mineralized tissues

Cremation, diagenesis, and bioapatite preservation

Burnt bone and teeth can undergo major structural and chemical alteration, but their mineral fraction may still preserve useful information. Research in this area examines cremated remains, dentine defects, strontium isotope signals, infrared crystallinity, pretreatment methods, and how heating or burial changes bioapatite [22,34–38].

Preservation is controlled by the mineral phase, crystal structure, carbonate content, porosity, heating history, and post-mortem alteration. Even modified biominerals can remain informative if their alteration pathways are understood [22,35–38].

These examples show that biomineralization is not only a topic in developmental biology or materials chemistry. It also underpins how researchers interpret human and animal remains, diagnose or reconstruct disease, evaluate implant integration, study childhood stress, analyse mobility, and assess preservation. In each case, the key object is a mineralized tissue whose structure and chemistry were shaped during life and may have been modified after death [22–44].

Materials science toolbox

How do we study biomineralization, and what can each method tell us?

No single method tells the whole story. Strong biomineralization studies combine morphology, phase, chemistry, organic content, hydration, mechanics, and time-resolved observations.

A sensible study workflow

  1. Start non-destructively: optical imaging, micro-CT, Raman/FTIR vibrational spectroscopy, or XRD where appropriate.
  2. Map structure and chemistry: SEM/EDX, Raman mapping, FTIR imaging, XRF, or electron microscopy.
  3. Go nanoscale: TEM, cryo-TEM, SAED, STEM-EDX/EELS, or AFM for interfaces and precursors.
  4. Test mechanism: reproduce simplified conditions in vitro, vary pH/additives/ions, and compare products to the biological material.
  5. Connect to function: combine structure with mechanical testing, dissolution behavior, or biological performance.
